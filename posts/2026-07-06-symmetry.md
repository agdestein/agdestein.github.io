# What symmetry buys a learned turbulence model

**One piece of work, three tellings** — the paper in full, the talk in 15
minutes, and this post to play with:

<PublicationList work="symmetry" />
<TalkList work="symmetry" :limit="1" />

Everyone building neural closures for turbulence has heard the advice: *bake
the symmetries of the Navier–Stokes equations into your network, and it will
be more accurate and generalize better.* It sounds so reasonable that it is
rarely tested. So we tested it — three networks, same inputs, same outputs,
same training data, from "no constraints at all" to "exactly equivariant by
construction". This post is the paper with the knobs exposed.

<SymFilterHero />

If you only remember three things:

<div class="symp-tldr">
  <ol>
    <li>All three architectures — constrained or not — converge to the <em>same</em> accuracy, and that shared floor is the <em>optimal</em> one-point closure, measurable directly from data with a histogram.</li>
    <li>Symmetry constraints don't lower the floor. They buy the trip to it with <strong>25× fewer parameters</strong>, reproducibly, where the unconstrained network is a lottery.</li>
    <li>Generalizing across Reynolds numbers was less about architecture than about <em>one missing input</em>: a filter-scale Reynolds number.</li>
  </ol>
</div>

## <span class="symp-act">Act I</span> The world below the cut

Simulating every eddy (direct numerical simulation, DNS) is gloriously
accurate and hopelessly expensive. Large-eddy simulation (LES) evolves a
*filtered* flow on an affordable grid, and pays with an unclosed term: the
**sub-filter stress** $\tau(u)$, the effect of everything the filter erased.
A *closure model* $m(\bar{u})$ must predict that stress from the resolved
field alone. Here is the bargain, on our actual data — drag the cut:

<SymSpectrumCut />

Classical closures (Smagorinsky, Clark, …) are physics-derived guesses at
that erased activity. The modern alternative is to train a neural network on
filtered DNS. This paper is about what happens when you do that with — and
without — symmetry constraints.

## <span class="symp-act">Act II</span> What a closure can promise

<div class="symp-stats">
  <SymStat value="48" label="rotations + reflections survive the grid" detail="of the infinitely many in the continuum — the octahedral group" tone="blue" />
  <SymStat value="≈ 7%" label="equivariance violation of our trained, unconstrained MLP" tone="orange" />
  <SymStat value="0" label="accuracy cost of enforcing the surviving symmetries" tone="green" />
</div>

The Navier–Stokes equations don't care how you orient your axes: rotate the
flow, and the physics — including the exact sub-filter stress — rotates with
it. A closure respects this if **predicting then rotating** equals **rotating
then predicting**: *equivariance*. Try to break it (the mismatch is computed
honestly from the two paths, not scripted):

<SymEquivariance />

The second toggle is what an unconstrained network is free to learn:
perfectly fine on the training data, but reading tensor components off a
fixed coordinate frame — a preferred direction the physics doesn't have. The
toy exaggerates; our actual trained MLP violates equivariance by a subtler
but very measurable ≈ 7%.

### The grid breaks the circle

The *continuous* equations are symmetric under **every** rotation — but your
simulation lives on a grid. Drag the slider and watch which rotations map the
grid onto itself:

<SymRotationGrid />

Only multiples of 90° and mirror flips survive: in 3D, the 48-element
**octahedral group**. A closure acting on grid data can be *exactly*
equivariant to those 48 — and to no more. Remember 48; it returns below as
the width of a network layer. The same audit, for every symmetry of the
equations:

<div class="symp-cards">
  <div class="symp-card symp-card-ok">
    <div class="symp-card-title">Survive the grid ✓</div>
    <div class="symp-card-body">Translations, time shifts, Galilean frame changes, and the 48 octahedral roto-reflections. Exact symmetries of the discrete equations — enforcing them costs nothing.</div>
  </div>
  <div class="symp-card symp-card-mid">
    <div class="symp-card-title">Survive partially ~</div>
    <div class="symp-card-body">Scaling: no fixed grid maps to itself under rescaling, but a closure can still <em>transform correctly</em> across rescaled systems. The viscous part of this returns in Act IV.</div>
  </div>
  <div class="symp-card symp-card-bad">
    <div class="symp-card-title">Broken ✗</div>
    <div class="symp-card-body">Continuous rotations (reduced to the 48) and accelerating frames (broken by the fixed forcing). A closure can still enforce them as a <em>continuum</em> ideal — should it?</div>
  </div>
</div>

### Three networks, one contract

All three data-driven closures sign the same contract — local, normalized
gradient in, dimensionally consistent stress out:

$$
m(\bar{A}) \;=\; \Delta^2 |\bar{A}|^2 \; f\!\left(\bar{A} / |\bar{A}|\right),
$$

with filter width $\Delta$ and velocity-gradient tensor $\bar{A}$. Gradients
as inputs make every model Galilean-invariant for free; the prefactor gives
the output the units and scaling of a stress. The three differ *only* in how
they treat rotations:

<div class="symp-cards">
  <div class="symp-card symp-card-mlp">
    <div class="symp-card-title">MLP — enforce nothing</div>
    <div class="symp-card-body">A plain multi-layer perceptron, 9 gradient components in, 6 stress components out. Whatever equivariance it has, it learned from data. The control group.</div>
  </div>
  <div class="symp-card symp-card-gcnn">
    <div class="symp-card-title">G-CNN — constrain the weights</div>
    <div class="symp-card-body">Each hidden layer carries 48 copies of its channels, one per octahedral element, weights tied so that rotating the input provably permutes the copies. Exactly equivariant in floating point.</div>
  </div>
  <div class="symp-card symp-card-tbnn">
    <div class="symp-card-title">TBNN — constrain the features</div>
    <div class="symp-card-body">The stress is expanded in 7 tensors that rotate correctly no matter what; the network only predicts their 7 scalar coefficients from 5 rotation-invariant inputs.</div>
  </div>
</div>

Constrain the architecture, constrain the representation, or constrain
nothing: the two constrained routes reach exact equivariance from opposite
directions — and, it turns out, the same destination.

## <span class="symp-act">Act III</span> Same floor, very different price

<div class="symp-stats">
  <SymStat value="0.439" label="the error floor nobody gets below" detail="measured from data with a histogram — no network, no training" />
  <SymStat value="25×" label="more parameters before the MLP catches up" detail="≈ 3000 vs ≈ 120 — and a seed lottery on the way" tone="orange" />
  <SymStat value="0.6–2.3%" label="how close all three saturate to the floor" tone="green" />
</div>

Train all three at a range of sizes; plot error against parameter count:

<SymSaturationChart />

Two things to see. The constrained models are *already saturated at ~120
parameters* — their built-in structure leaves that little to learn — while
the MLP starts worse than the 50-year-old Clark model and needs ~3000 to
catch up, with an order-of-magnitude wider seed-to-seed spread on the way.
And the more interesting one: *nobody gets below the dashed line.*

### Build the best possible closure yourself

That dashed line is not a fit to the networks — it is measured independently.
The best possible one-point predictor is a conditional average: over all flow
states sharing this local gradient, the *mean* stress (the **optimal
closure** of Langford & Moser). Averages need no training. Sort the data
into bins and watch it appear:

<SymCondMean />

The paper's version bins all five invariant inputs instead of one: 65 536
bins land at an error of **0.439** — and a *single* bin, i.e. seven constant
coefficients, already lands at 0.442. The saturated networks sit within
0.6–2.3% of that floor. The remaining 44% error is not a failure of the
networks: filtering destroys information, and no function of a single-point
gradient — neural or otherwise — can recover it.

<details class="symp-fold">
<summary>Footnote we enjoyed: equivariance for free, after the fact</summary>

Averaging the trained MLP's predictions over the 48 octahedral elements makes
it *exactly* equivariant at zero accuracy cost. Its 7% symmetry violation was
wasted degrees of freedom, not useful ones — the unconstrained optimum was
already statistically equivariant, because the training data was.

</details>

### Energy flows both ways

A-priori stress error is a laboratory metric. Plugged into an actual LES (the
*a posteriori* toggle above), the learned closures tie at a solution error of
≈ 0.35, ahead of Clark (0.36), dynamic Smagorinsky (0.39), and no model
(0.43). But the aggregate number hides the most physical difference — what
each closure does with *energy*:

<SymBackscatter />

The exact stress mostly drains energy from the resolved scales, but at 22% of
grid points it pushes energy *backwards* — **backscatter**, the shaded
region. Eddy-viscosity models stop dead at zero, by construction. The learned
closure reproduces both tails, yet covers only about half the reference
backscatter fraction: the fingerprint of having learned a conditional
average. Means are milder than samples.

## <span class="symp-act">Act IV</span> One number to rule the Reynolds number

<div class="symp-stats">
  <SymStat value="1.35× → 0.75×" label="dissipation drift of a Reynolds-blind closure" detail="over- to under-dissipative as the flow leaves the training band" tone="orange" />
  <SymStat value="1" label="extra input that fixes the calibration" detail="the only scaling-invariant scalar available: a filter-scale Reynolds number" tone="blue" />
  <SymStat value="2.07× → 1.80×" label="what it recovers on a truly different flow" detail="Taylor–Green vortex, tightest filter — regime ≠ flow kind" tone="pink" />
</div>

The quiet limitation of the contract in Act II: it contains no viscosity. The
closures are **Reynolds-blind** — their dissipation calibration is pinned to
the training regime. The symmetry analysis itself predicts the fix: the full
scaling symmetry of Navier–Stokes involves the viscosity, and the only
scaling-invariant scalar you can build from the available quantities is a
filter-scale Reynolds number,

$$
\operatorname{Re}_\Delta = \frac{\Delta^2 \| \bar{A} \|}{\nu},
$$

where $\| \bar{A} \|$ is a global norm of the resolved gradient — one number
per flow, unlike the pointwise $|\bar{A}|$ in the contract.

Train across viscosities and filter widths, with and without
$\operatorname{Re}_\Delta$ as one extra input; test on held-out Reynolds
numbers well outside the training range. Flip the toggle:

<SymRedeltaTrend />

Blind, the story is a slide from over- to under-dissipative. With the one
extra input, calibration holds out into extrapolation. Meanwhile the *stress
error* tab barely moves: the tensor **structure** of the stress is nearly
Reynolds-independent — what changes is only *how much* energy lives below the
filter, and one scalar carries exactly that. Notice the three architectures
never separate: the gain comes from the input, not from equivariance.

### A flow it has never seen

<figure class="symp-video">
  <video src="/posts/symmetry/tgv.mp4" poster="/posts/symmetry/tgv0.webp" autoplay loop muted playsinline controls></video>
  <figcaption>A decaying Taylor–Green vortex: laminar roll-up, transition, turbulent decay — nothing like the forced training soup. Vorticity on a slice, from the paper's out-of-distribution study.</figcaption>
</figure>

The honest caveat. On this flow the blind closures over-dissipate by ~2×, and
$\operatorname{Re}_\Delta$ recovers only part of it (2.07× → 1.80× at the
tightest filter): a single global scalar tells the closure *which Reynolds
regime* it is in, not *which kind of flow*. The learned closures do stay
stable and keep the lowest errors through the transition — while Clark
diverges outright at the wider filters.

## <span class="symp-act">Epilogue</span> The verdict

<div class="symp-verdict">
  <table>
    <thead>
      <tr><th>Equivariance was advertised as…</th><th></th><th>What the controlled test says</th></tr>
    </thead>
    <tbody>
      <tr><td>more accurate</td><td class="symp-no">✗</td><td>all three architectures converge to the same measurable floor</td></tr>
      <tr><td>better at generalizing</td><td class="symp-no">✗</td><td>crossing Reynolds numbers came from one extra input, not the architecture</td></tr>
      <tr><td>—</td><td class="symp-yes">✓</td><td><strong>parameter-efficient:</strong> saturated at ~120 parameters, 25× fewer than the MLP</td></tr>
      <tr><td>—</td><td class="symp-yes">✓</td><td><strong>reliable:</strong> reproducible across seeds where the small MLP is a lottery</td></tr>
      <tr><td>—</td><td class="symp-yes">✓</td><td><strong>physically consistent:</strong> exact in floating point — no spurious frame-dependent forces</td></tr>
    </tbody>
  </table>
</div>

If you are building a closure, the recipe this leaves behind:

- **Measure your floor first.** The conditional-mean estimate costs nothing
  and tells you the best any pointwise model can do *before* you design one.
- **At the floor, capacity is wasted.** The only way down is richer inputs —
  neighborhoods, history, or scalars like $\operatorname{Re}_\Delta$ that
  carry genuinely new information (the +Re TBNN measurably dips *below* the
  one-point floor out of distribution).
- **Buy symmetry for the right reasons.** Efficiency, reproducibility, and
  physical consistency are real currencies — just different ones than
  advertised.

<div class="symp-quote">
  A closure that knows what it's missing works where it has never been.
</div>
